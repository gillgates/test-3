Who Was Alan Turing?

Alan Mathison Turing (1912–1954) was a British mathematician and logician whose ideas gave us the modern concept of computation. In 1936 he showed that a simple abstract device what we now call the Turing Machine could carry out any algorithmic process. That idea established the formal limits of computation and launched the study of what machines can, in principle, compute. During World War II, Turing worked at Bletchley Park and played a leading role in breaking the Enigma cipher. His engineering and logical approaches to cryptanalysis accelerated codebreaking efforts and had profound wartime impact. After the war he wrote about whether machines could think and proposed the Turing Test a practical, behaviour based way to ask whether a machine’s responses are indistinguishable from a human’s. Turing’s life ended in tragedy. In 1952 he was prosecuted for his homosexuality and subjected to chemical treatment. He died in 1954. Today his scientific legacy is recognised worldwide, and his personal story is a reminder about the costs of intolerance.
